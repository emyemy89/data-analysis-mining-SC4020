<title>Cosmology</title><text>[[File:Hubble - infant galaxy.jpg|thumb|Hubble - infant galaxy]]  '''Cosmology''' is the branch of [[astronomy]] that deals with the origin, structure, evolution and space-time relationships of the [[universe]].<ref>{{cite web|url=https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/cosmology|title=Definition of COSMOLOGY|website=www.merriam-webster.com}}</ref><ref>{{cite book|title=Collins Dictionary of Astronomy|publisher=HarperCollins|date=2012|origyear=2006|edition=Fifth|first1=John|last1=Daintith|first2=William|last2=Gould|isbn=9780007918485|page=96}}</ref> [[NASA]] defines cosmology as "The study of the structure and changes in the present universe".<ref name="NASA">{{cite web|url=http://genesismission.jpl.nasa.gov/educate/scimodule/Cosmogony/CosmogonyPDF/CosCosmolTT.pdf|title=NASA education|publisher=}}</ref> Another definition of cosmology is "the study of the universe, and humanity's place in it".<ref name=bs>{{cite web|url=http://www.bbc.co.uk/schools/gcsebitesize/rs/|title=Christianity: beliefs about creation and evolution|publisher=BBC|accessdate=2011-11-03}}</ref>  Modern cosmology is dominated by the [[Big Bang]] theory, which brings together observational astronomy and [[particle physics]].<ref>{{cite web|url=https://en.oxforddictionaries.com/definition/cosmology|title=cosmology - Definition of cosmology in English by Oxford Dictionaries|website=Oxford Dictionaries - English}}</ref>   Though the word ''cosmology'' is recent (first used in 1730 in [[Christian Wolff]]'s ''Cosmologia Generalis''), the study of the universe has a long history.  ==History== Until recently, people thought that the [[universe]] was only the [[Milky Way]] [[galaxy]]. They thought this because they could only see the planets up to [[Saturn (planet)|Saturn]] and [[star]]s. With the invention of the [[telescope]], we could see more of the universe. Even in the 20th century, people thought that the Milky Way was the entire universe. With the [[Hubble Space Telescope]], people could see things far away.  Modern cosmology is considered to have started in [[1915|1917]] with the final paper of [[Albert Einstein|Albert Einstein's]] [[General relativity|theory of general relativity]]. This paper was known as "Cosmological Considerations of the General Theory of Relativity"<ref>{{Cite journal|last=Einstein|first=A.|date=1952|title=Cosmological considerations on the general theory of relativity|url=https://ui.adsabs.harvard.edu/abs/1952prel.book..175E/abstract|journal=The Principle of Relativity. Dover Books on Physics. June 1|language=en|pages=175188}}</ref> which made physicists start to change the assumption that the Universe never changed. When a scientific discipline begins to change an idea that is believed by many people, it is known as a [[Paradigm Shift|paradigm shift]].<ref>{{Cite journal|date=1963-04|title=The Structure of Scientific Revolutions. By <italic>Thomas S. Kuhn</italic>. (Chicago: University of Chicago Press. 1962. Pp. xv, 172. $4.00.)|url=http://dx.doi.org/10.1086/ahr/68.3.700|journal=The American Historical Review|doi=10.1086/ahr/68.3.700|issn=1937-5239}}</ref> During this paradigm shift, the [[Great Debate]] took place. Many [[Scientist|scientists]] [[Debate|debated]] if there were other galaxies. The debate ended when [[Edwin Hubble]] found [[Cepheid variable|Cepheid Variables]] in the [[Andromeda galaxy|Andromeda Galaxy]] in [[1926]].<ref>{{Cite journal|last=Falk|first=Dan|date=2009-03|title=Review: The Day We Found the Universe by Marcia Bartusiak|url=http://dx.doi.org/10.1016/s0262-4079(09)60809-5|journal=New Scientist|volume=201|issue=2700|pages=45|doi=10.1016/s0262-4079(09)60809-5|issn=0262-4079}}</ref><ref>{{Cite journal|last=Hubble|first=E. P.|date=1926-12|title=Extragalactic nebulae.|url=http://adsabs.harvard.edu/doi/10.1086/143018|journal=The Astrophysical Journal|language=en|volume=64|pages=321|doi=10.1086/143018|issn=0004-637X}}</ref>  The Big Bang model was then created by [[Belgium|Belgian]] [[priest]], [[Georges Lematre]] in [[1927]].<ref>{{Cite journal|last=Martin|first=G.|date=1883|title=G. DELSAULX.  Sur une proprit de la diffraction des ondes planes;  Annales de la Socit scientifique de Bruxelles; 1882|url=http://dx.doi.org/10.1051/jphystap:018830020017501|journal=Journal de Physique Thorique et Applique|volume=2|issue=1|pages=175175|doi=10.1051/jphystap:018830020017501|issn=0368-3893}}</ref> This was supported by Edwin Hubble's [[Discovery doctrine|discovery]] of the [[Red shift|redshift]] in 1929<ref>{{Cite journal|last=Hubble|first=Edwin|date=1929-03-15|title=A RELATION BETWEEN DISTANCE AND RADIAL VELOCITY AMONG EXTRA-GALACTIC NEBULAE|url=https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC522427/|journal=Proceedings of the National Academy of Sciences of the United States of America|volume=15|issue=3|pages=168173|issn=0027-8424|pmid=16577160}}</ref> and later by the discovery of [[cosmic microwave background radiation]]. This was found by [[Arzo Penzias]] and [[Robert Wilson|Robert Woodrow Wilson]] in [[1964]].<ref>{{Cite journal|last=Penzias|first=A. A.|last2=Wilson|first2=R. W.|date=1965-07|title=A Measurement of Excess Antenna Temperature at 4080 Mc/s.|url=http://adsabs.harvard.edu/doi/10.1086/148307|journal=The Astrophysical Journal|language=en|volume=142|pages=419|doi=10.1086/148307|issn=0004-637X}}</ref>   All of these discoveries have been further supported throughout the 20th century. A redshift survey is a survey that looks for redshifts.
