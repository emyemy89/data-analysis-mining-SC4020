'''Mercury''' is the smallest [[planet]] in the [[Solar System]].<ref>{{cite web |url= http://nineplanets.org/mercury.html |title=Mercury Facts {{!}} Information, History, Location, Size & Definition |first= |last= |work= nineplanets.org |year=July 2020 |origyear= last update |accessdate=17 August 2020}}</ref><ref>{{cite web |url= http://www.bbc.co.uk/science/space/solarsystem/sun_and_planets/mercury_(planet) |title=BBC Solar System - Mercury: A tortured world close to our blazing Sun |first= |last= |work= bbc.co.uk |year=2012 |origyear= last update |accessdate=7 March 2012}}</ref><ref>[[Pluto]] was once thought to be the smallest, but, as of 2006, Pluto is now known as a [[dwarf planet]].</ref>  It is the closest planet to the sun.<ref name=sciencefact>{{citation|title=Space Science: Journey to the Innermost Planet|newspaper=Scientific American|pages=26-31|date=March 2011|location=New York|volume=304|issue=3|author1=Murchie, Scott L.|author2=Vervack Jr., Ronald J.|author3=Anderson, Brian J.}}</ref> It makes one [[orbit|trip]] around the Sun once every 87.969 days.<ref name="nssdcMercury" /><ref>[http://image.gsfc.nasa.gov/poetry/venus/VTratesExt.html]</ref> Mercury is bright when it is visible from [[Earth]], ranging from 2.0 to 5.5 in [[apparent magnitude]]. It cannot be easily seen as it is usually too close to the Sun.  Because Mercury is normally lost in the glare of the Sun, Mercury can only be seen in the morning or evening [[twilight]]<ref name="hiw">{{cite book|title=How it Works Book of Space|date=2010|publisher=Imagine Publishing|isbn=9781906078829|page=36-37}}</ref> or during a [[solar eclipse]].  Less is known about Mercury than about other planets of our Solar System.  [[Telescope]]s<nowiki/> on the Earth show only a small, bright crescent, and putting a [[Satellite (artificial)|satellite]] in orbit around it is difficult.  The first of two [[spacecraft]] to visit the planet was [[Mariner 10|Mariner&nbsp;10]],<ref>[http://nssdc.gsfc.nasa.gov/nmc/masterCatalog.do?sc=1973-085A]</ref> which mapped only about 45% of the planets surface from 1974 to 1975. The second is the [[MESSENGER]] spacecraft, which finished mapping the planet in March 2013.  Mercury looks like Earth's [[Moon]].  It has many [[crater]]s and areas of smooth plains, no moons around it and no [[atmosphere]] as we know it.  However, Mercury does have an extremely thin atmosphere, known as an [[exosphere]].<ref name=sciencefact />  Unlike Earth's Moon, Mercury has a large [[iron]] [[planetary core|core]], which gives off a [[magnetic field]] about 1% as strong as that of the [[Earth]].<ref>{{cite web |url=http://www-spc.igpp.ucla.edu/personnel/russell/papers/merc_mag/|title=Mercury magnetic field|publisher=C. T. Russell & J. G. Luhmann|accessdate=2007-03-16}}</ref> It is a very dense planet due to the large size of its core. Surface temperatures can be anywhere from about 90 to {{nowrap|700 [[Kelvin|K]]}} (183&nbsp;C to 427&nbsp;C, 297&nbsp;F to 801&nbsp;F),<ref name="ESAs&t">{{cite web|url=http://sci.esa.int/science-e/www/category/index.cfm?fcategoryid=4586|title=Background Science|publisher=European Space Agency|accessdate=2008-05-23}}</ref> with the [[subsolar point]] being the hottest and the bottoms of craters near the [[Geographical pole|poles]] being the coldest.  Known sightings of Mercury date back to at least the first millennium BC. Before the 4th century BC, Greek [[astronomers]] thought that Mercury was two different objects: one able to be seen only at sunrise, which they called [[Apollo]]; the other that was only able to be seen at sunset, which they called [[Hermes]].<ref name="Dunne">{{cite book|title=The Voyage of Mariner&nbsp;10  Mission to Venus and Mercury|author=Dunne, J. A. and Burgess, E.|chapterurl=http://history.nasa.gov/SP-424/ch1.htm|publisher=NASA History Office|year=1978|chapter=Chapter One|url=http://history.nasa.gov/SP-424/}}</ref> The English name for the planet is from the [[Ancient Rome|Romans]], who named it after the Roman [[Roman mythology|god]] [[Mercury (mythology)|Mercury]], which they thought to be the same as the Greek god [[Hermes]]. The symbol for Mercury is based on Hermes' [[Staff (stick)|staff]].<ref>{{cite book|title=Astronomy: A Textbook|first=John Charles|last=Duncan|year=1946|publisher=Harper & Brothers|pages=125|quote=The symbol for Mercury represents the Caduceus, a wand with two serpents twined around it, which was carried by the messenger of the gods.}}</ref>  Even though Mercury is the closest planet to the Sun, it is not the warmest. This is because it has no [[greenhouse effect]], so any heat that the Sun gives to it quickly escapes into space. The hottest planet is [[Venus]].<ref>{{cite web | url=http://www.bbc.co.uk/newsround/14143062 | title=CBBC Newsround | publisher=British Broadcasting Corporation | accessdate=February 4, 2012}}</ref>  == Inside Mercury == Mercury is one of four [[inner planet]]s in the [[Solar System]], and has a rocky body like the [[Earth]]. It is the smallest planet in the Solar System, with a [[radius]] of {{cvt|2439.7|km|mi}}.<ref name="nssdcMercury" /> Mercury is even smaller than some of the largest moons in the solar system, such as [[Ganymede]] and [[Titan (moon)|Titan]]. However, it has a greater mass than the largest moons in the solar system. Mercury is made of about 70% [[metal]]lic and 30% [[silicate]] material.<ref name="strom" >{{cite book |title=Exploring Mercury: The Iron Planet |url=https://archive.org/details/exploringmercury00stro |last=Strom |first=Strom, Robert G.  |author2=Sprague, Ann L. |year=2003 |page=[https://archive.org/details/exploringmercury00stro/page/52 52]  |isbn= 1852337311}}</ref> Mercury's [[density]] is the second highest in the Solar System at 5.427&nbsp;g/cm, only a little bit less than Earths.<ref name="nssdcMercury" />  ==Surface of Mercury== Mercury's [[surface]] looks similar to the surface of the Moon. It has [[Plain|plains]] that look like [[Lunar mare|mares]] and has lots of [[Crater|craters]].<ref>{{Cite web|title=Mercury's Core Molten, Radar Study Shows|url=https://www.nrao.edu/pr/2007/mercury/|access-date=2020-09-12|website=www.nrao.edu}}</ref> Mercury was hit by a lot of [[Comet|comets]] and [[Asteroid|asteroids]] 4.6 billion years ago. Mercury was also hit during a period called the [[Late Heavy Bombardment]].<ref>{{Cite journal|last=Strom|first=RobertG.|date=1979|title=Mercury: A post-Mariner 10 assessment|url=http://link.springer.com/10.1007/BF00221842|journal=Space Science Reviews|language=en|volume=24|issue=1|pages=|doi=10.1007/BF00221842|issn=0038-6308|via=}}</ref> Mercury has lots of craters because it does not have any [[atmosphere]] to slow objects down.<ref>{{Cite journal|last=Broadfoot|first=A. L.|last2=Kumar|first2=S.|last3=Belton|first3=M. J. S.|last4=McElroy|first4=M. It does have a thin [[exosphere]] that contains [[hydrogen]], [[helium]], [[oxygen]], [[sodium]], [[calcium]], [[potassium]].<ref>{{Cite journal|last=Domingue|first=Deborah L.|last2=Koehn|first2=Patrick L.|last3=Killen|first3=Rosemary M.|last4=Sprague|first4=Ann L.|last5=Sarantos|first5=Menelaos|last6=Cheng|first6=Andrew F.|last7=Bradley|first7=Eric T.|last8=McClintock|first8=William E.|date=2007|title=Mercurys Atmosphere: A Surface-Bounded Exosphere|url=http://link.springer.com/10.1007/s11214-007-9260-9|journal=Space Science Reviews|language=en|volume=131|issue=1-4|pages=161186|doi=10.1007/s11214-007-9260-9|issn=0038-6308|via=}}</ref><ref>{{Cite web|date=2015-11-06|title=Mercury Fact Sheet|url=https://web.archive.org/web/20151106171436/http://nssdc.gsfc.nasa.gov/planetary/factsheet/mercuryfact.html|access-date=2020-09-12|website=web.archive.org}}</ref> This exosphere is lost and replenished from lots of sources. Hydrogen and helium may come from the [[solar wind]]. [[Radioactive decay]] of [[Chemical element|elements]] inside the crust of Mercury is another source of helium, and also sodium and potassium.<ref>{{Cite book|url=https://www.worldcat.org/oclc/18557585|title=Mercury|date=1988|publisher=University of Arizona Press|others=Vilas, Faith., Chapman, Clark R., Matthews, Mildred Shapley., American Astronomical Society. Commission 16., Mercury Conference (1986 : Tucson, Ariz.)|isbn=0-8165-1085-7|location=Tucson|oclc=18557585}}</ref>  ==Orbit and rotation== [[File:Mercuryorbitsolarsystem.gif|thumb|right|Animation of Mercury and the Earth orbiting the Sun]] Mercury has the most [[Orbital eccentricity|eccentric orbit]] of all the [[Planet|planets]]; its eccentricity is 0.21. Its distance from the Sun ranges from 46,000,000 to 70,000,000 km (29,000,000 to 43,000,000 mi). It takes 87.969 Earth days to go around the [[Sun]].<ref>{{Cite journal|last=Van Hoolst|first=T.|date=2003|title=Mercury's tides and interior structure|url=http://doi.wiley.com/10.1029/2003JE002126|journal=Journal of Geophysical Research|language=en|volume=108|issue=E11|pages=5121|doi=10.1029/2003JE002126|issn=0148-0227}}</ref> Mercury's [[axial tilt]] is 0.027 degrees which is best measurement of the axial tilt.<ref>{{Cite book|url=https://www.worldcat.org/oclc/41319901|title=Cosmic perspectives in space physics|last=Biswas, S. They are:  ===''Mariner 10''=== {{Main|Mariner 10}} [[File:Mariner_10.jpg|thumb|right|Mariner 10]] The first [[spacecraft]] to visit Mercury was NASA's Mariner 10. It stayed in Mercury's orbit from 19741975.<ref>{{Cite web|title=ch1|url=https://history.nasa.gov/SP-424/ch1.htm|access-date=2020-09-12|website=history.nasa.gov}}</ref> Mariner 10 provided the first close-up [[Picture|pictures]] of Mercury's surface. It showed many types of geological features, such as the [[Crater|craters]].<ref>{{Cite web|last=Science@NASA|title=NASA - 2006 Transit of Mercury|url=https://www.nasa.gov/vision/universe/solarsystem/20oct_transitofmercury.html|access-date=2020-09-12|website=www.nasa.gov|language=en}}</ref> Unfortunately, the same face of the planet was day at each time Mariner 10 flew close to Mercury. This made close observation of both sides of the planet impossible. In the end, less than 45% of the planet's surface was mapped.<ref>{{Cite web|title=ESA Science & Technology - Missions to Mercury|url=https://sci.esa.int/web/bepicolombo/-/56015-missions-to-mercury|access-date=2020-09-12|website=sci.esa.int}}</ref><ref>{{Cite web|title=USATODAY.com - MESSENGER to test theory of shrinking Mercury|url=https://usatoday30.usatoday.com/tech/news/2004-08-16-mercury-may-shrink_x.htm|access-date=2020-09-12|website=usatoday30.usatoday.com}}</ref>  The Mariner 10 came close to Mercury three times.<ref>{{Cite web|title=mariner|url=https://history.nasa.gov/SP-423/mariner.htm|access-date=2020-09-12|website=history.nasa.gov}}</ref> At the first time, instruments found a [[magnetic field]], which surprised [[Planetary geology|planetary geologists]] because Mercury's rotation was too slow to generate a magnetic field. The second time was mainly used to take pictures of Mercury's surface. At the third time, more information about the magnetic field were obtained. It showed that the planet's magnetic field is much like Earth's.<ref>{{Cite journal|last=Ness|first=NormanF.|date=1978|title=Mercury: Magnetic field and interior|url=http://link.springer.com/10.1007/BF00240907|journal=Space Science Reviews|language=en|volume=21|issue=5|pages=|doi=10.1007/BF00240907|issn=0038-6308|via=}}</ref><ref>{{Cite journal|last=Aharonson|first=Oded|last2=Zuber|first2=Maria T|last3=Solomon|first3=Sean C|date=2004|title=Crustal remanence in an internally magnetized non-uniform shell: a possible source for Mercurys magnetic field?|url=https://linkinghub.elsevier.com/retrieve/pii/S0012821X03006824|journal=Earth and Planetary Science Letters|language=en|volume=218|issue=3-4|pages=261268|doi=10.1016/S0012-821X(03)00682-4|via=}}</ref>  On March 24, 1975, just eight days after its final close approach, Mariner 10 ran out of [[fuel]]. Because its orbit could no longer be controlled, mission controllers instructed the probe to shut down.<ref>{{Cite web|title=ch8|url=https://history.nasa.gov/SP-424/ch8.htm|access-date=2020-09-12|website=history.nasa.gov}}</ref> Mariner 10 is thought to still be orbiting the Sun.<ref>{{Cite web|title=NASA - NSSDCA - Spacecraft - Details|url=https://nssdc.gsfc.nasa.gov/nmc/spacecraft/display.action?id=1973-085A|access-date=2020-09-12|website=nssdc.gsfc.nasa.gov}}</ref>  ===''MESSENGER''=== {{Main|MESSENGER}} [[File:MESSENGER_-_spacecraft_at_mercury_-_atmercury_lg.jpg|thumb|right|MESSENGER]]The second satellite to reach Mercury is NASA's MESSENGER. It stands for MErcury Surface, Space ENvironment, GEochemistry, and Ranging. It was launched on August 3, 2004. It made a fly-by of [[Earth]] in August 2005. It made another fly-by of [[Venus]] in October 2006.<ref>{{Cite web|title=MESSENGER Engine Burn Puts Spacecraft on Track for Venus|url=http://www.spaceref.com/news/viewsr.html?pid=18956|access-date=2020-09-14|website=www.spaceref.com}}</ref> It made its first fly-by of Mercury happened on January 14, 2008, a second on October 6, 2008, and a third on September 29, 2009.<ref>{{Cite web|date=2013-05-13|title=MESSENGER: MErcury Surface, Space ENvironment, GEochemistry, and Ranging|url=https://web.archive.org/web/20130513080731/http://messenger.jhuapl.edu/gallery/sciencePhotos/image.php?gallery_id=2&image_id=115|access-date=2020-09-14|website=web.archive.org}}</ref><ref>{{Cite web|last=|first=|date=|title=MESSENGER Gains Critical Gravity Assist for Mercury Orbital Observations|url=https://web.archive.org/web/20130510175510/http://messenger.jhuapl.edu/news_room/details.php?id=136|url-status=live|archive-url=|archive-date=|access-date=2020-09-14|website=web.archive.org}}</ref> Most of the [[hemisphere]] not mapped by ''Mariner 10'' was mapped during these fly-bys. The satellite entered an [[elliptical orbit]] around the planet on March 18, 2011. The first image of Mercury orbiting the Sun was gotten on March 29, 2011.<ref>{{Cite web|date=2013-05-13|title=MESSENGER: MErcury Surface, Space ENvironment, GEochemistry, and Ranging|url=https://web.archive.org/web/20130513080731/http://messenger.jhuapl.edu/gallery/sciencePhotos/image.php?gallery_id=2&image_id=115|access-date=2020-09-14|website=web.archive.org}}</ref>  MESSENGER was made to study Mercury's high [[density]], the history of Mercury's [[geology]], its magnetic field, the [[structure]] of its core, whether it has ice at its poles, and where its thin atmosphere comes from. ''MESSENGER'' crashed into Mercury's surface on April 30, 2015.<ref>{{Cite web|last=March 2015|first=Mike Wall 29|title=NASA Mercury Probe Trying to Survive for Another Month|url=https://www.space.com/28948-messenger-mercury-probe-final-days.html|access-date=2020-09-14|website=Space.com|language=en}}</ref><ref>{{Cite news|last=Chang|first=Kenneth|date=2015-04-27|title=NASAs Messenger Mission Is Set to Crash Into Mercury|language=en-US|work=The New York Times|url=https://www.nytimes.com/2015/04/28/science/nasas-messenger-mission-is-set-to-crash-into-mercury.html|access-date=2020-09-14|issn=0362-4331}}</ref><ref>{{Cite news|last=Corum|first=Jonathan|date=2015-04-30|title=Messengers Collision Course With Mercury|language=en-US|work=The New York Times|url=https://www.nytimes.com/interactive/2015/04/30/science/space/messenger-collides-with-mercury.html,%20https://www.nytimes.com/interactive/2015/04/30/science/space/messenger-collides-with-mercury.html|access-date=2020-09-14|issn=0362-4331}}</ref>  ===''Bepicolombo''=== {{Main|Bepicolombo}} [[File:BepiColombo_spacecraft_model.png|thumb|right|Bepicolombo]]The [[European Space Agency]] and the [[Japanese Space Agency]] developed and launched a joint mission called ''BepiColombo.'' It will [[orbit]] Mercury with two probes: one to map the planet and the other to study its [[magnetosphere]].<ref>{{Cite web|title=ESA gives go-ahead to build BepiColombo|url=http://www.esa.int/Science_Exploration/Space_Science/ESA_gives_go-ahead_to_build_BepiColombo|access-date=2020-09-14|website=www.esa.int|language=en}}</ref> It was launched on October 20, 2018. ''BepiColombo'' is expected to reach Mercury in 2025.<ref>{{Cite web|title=ESA Science & Technology - Fact Sheet|url=https://sci.esa.int/web/bepicolombo/-/47346-fact-sheet|access-date=2020-09-14|website=sci.esa.int}}</ref> It will release the probe that will study the magnetosphere into an [[elliptical orbit]].
